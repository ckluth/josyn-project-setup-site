ADR-0007: Succession & replaceability

  • Date: 2026-07-18
  • Status: proposed
  • Promoted from: Idea 0005: Succession & replaceability
  • Tags: succession, culture, governance

Context

The project is deliberately designed so the driver can step back and the new generation owns it — knowledge and authority not bound to a person. This is explicitly not about the driver: when the work is done he wants to disappear cleanly and let the next generation take over. Key-person dependency would defeat both the collaboration goal and the beacon goal.

The seed idea left four open questions, which this ADR settles:

  • What does "done" mean for a clean hand-over of ownership?
  • How does authority (accepting decisions, stewarding the method) transfer?
  • How do we surface and remove single-points-of-knowledge?
  • How do the young architects grow into owners along the way, not at the end?

This ADR decides the stance and mechanism of succession; concrete timing, who holds which authority when, and specific rotation choices are refined as the practice matures.

Decision Drivers

  • Continuity must survive the founder's exit — a culture that collapses when he steps back has failed its own purpose.
  • Authority and knowledge must not concentrate in one person (the driver included) — key-person dependency is the failure mode to design out.
  • Ownership is more durable when earned and grown continuously than when transferred in a single late event.
  • The method already externalizes reasoning into plain markdown — succession should lean on that, inventing nothing new.
  • Consistency with the adoption stance (ADR-0006) and the governance ladder (ADR-0003).

Considered Options

  • A — Continuous earned delegation via the trail. Ownership accrues from the start: the new generation authors and accepts real artifacts, authority is delegated incrementally as it is demonstrated, and the decision-trail keeps knowledge out of any single head. "Done" is a demonstrated capability.
  • B — Big-bang hand-over at the end. The driver holds authority and knowledge until a final transfer event. Legible but brittle — it manufactures exactly the key-person dependency it is meant to end, and concentrates risk at one moment.
  • C — Informal / no explicit succession. Assume ownership diffuses on its own. Cheapest, but leaves single-points-of-knowledge unsurfaced and the hand-over undefined — succession by hope, not design.

Why A. It makes ownership genuine and durable by growing it continuously rather than transferring it late, and it reuses the trail as the anti-key-person mechanism. B recreates the dependency it aims to remove and concentrates risk; C leaves the core questions unanswered.

Proposed decision

  1. "Done" is a demonstrated capability, not a date or ceremony. Hand-over is complete when the trail runs without the driver in any critical path: the new generation authors ideas, ADRs, and plans, accepts decisions, and stewards the method unaided for a sustained stretch — and the driver's absence changes nothing. Readiness is shown, not scheduled.

  2. Authority transfers by gradual, earned delegation — not a single handoff. Acceptance authority and method-stewardship move to the young architects incrementally as they demonstrate them, using the ADR-0003 governance ladder. The driver deliberately relinquishes the last word, retreating to advisor and finally to nothing. The direction is one-way: authority only ever moves outward.

  3. The decision-trail is the anti-key-person mechanism. Everything of weight lives in plain, human-readable markdown — nothing critical stays in one head. Any "only X knows this" is treated as a defect to be written down. Authorship is actively rotated so no area acquires a sole owner; concentrated knowledge is surfaced and dissolved as normal hygiene, not a late audit.

  4. Young architects own real work from the start, growing continuously. They own genuine ADRs and plans from the outset — not shadowing — with responsibility widening as conviction and capability grow, consistent with the participation lever in ADR-0006. Because ownership accrues along the way, there is nothing left to transfer at the end — the hand-over is the sum of many small ones already made.

Consequences

  • Ownership is durable and founder-independent — the point of the exercise; the trade is that succession is gradual and uneven, with no fixed completion date.
  • "Done" as a demonstrated capability resists gaming by ceremony but makes the milestone a judgment call — read from the trail's actual unaided use, not declared.
  • Rotating authorship costs some short-term efficiency (the fastest author is not always the one who should write it) in exchange for removing single-points-of-knowledge — an accepted trade.
  • One-way delegation means the driver progressively gives up control by design; this is deliberate and consistent with adoption by conviction (ADR-0006), not decree.
  • This ADR sets the succession stance; concrete sequencing — which authority moves when, exactly how rotation is scheduled, and when the driver steps fully back — is refined as the practice matures.
