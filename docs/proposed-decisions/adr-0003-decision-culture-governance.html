ADR-0003: Decision culture & governance

  • Date: 2026-07-17
  • Status: proposed
  • Promoted from: Idea 0003: Decision culture & governance
  • Tags: governance, decision-driven, culture

Context

The cultural core of this project's shift is decisions by reason, not by boss. The decision-trail method is the shared instrument, but a method alone does not make a culture: it must become the team's way of deciding, not the founder's. The open questions are who accepts a decision, how genuine disagreement is resolved by argument, when (if ever) an authority's "last word" is legitimate, and how the trail stays honest.

If governance quietly defaults to authority, the agentic / decision-driven ambition is hollow. The mechanism must let the best argument win and leave a legible trail.

Decision Drivers

  • The best argument should win — authority is a fallback, not the mechanism.
  • Every decision must leave a legible, honest trail.
  • Deadlock must have a bounded, predictable resolution so work never stalls.
  • The escalation path must exist but be rarely used — a safety valve, not a routine.

Considered Options

  • A — Consensus by reason, with a bounded escalation ladder when consensus fails. The team decides in the open; only a genuine no-consensus deadlock hands the decision to a named authority.
  • B — A single decision owner accepts every ADR (advised by the team). Simple, but makes authority the default mechanism — the opposite of "by reason."
  • C — Pure consensus, everyone must agree. Honest but brittle: a lone holdout can stall the project indefinitely, with no bounded resolution.

Why A. It makes reason the default and authority the exception, while still guaranteeing that a deadlock resolves in bounded time. B inverts the culture; C has no deadlock exit.

Proposed decision

  1. Default: team consensus by reason. Decisions are made in the open through the decision-trail — ADRs carrying Decision Drivers and Considered Options so the best argument wins and the reasoning stays legible. Acceptance is a team act, not a single person's prerogative.
  2. Escalation ladder, used only when consensus genuinely fails:
    1. Team consensus (the default and by far the common case).
    2. Technical Lead decides — the effective last authority when the team cannot converge by argument.
    3. Head of Dev — the ultimate backstop, expected never to be needed.
  3. Disagreement is resolved by argument, in the open. A genuine dispute is worked through the ADR itself (options weighed, drivers stated); escalation up the ladder is the exception, invoked only after reasoned discussion has failed to converge — never as a shortcut around it.
  4. The trail stays honest. Decisions are recorded as they are made, not retrofitted: an ADR precedes the work it governs, and a question that surfaces during execution returns to the decision stage (patch / amend / new ADR) rather than being backfilled after the fact.

Consequences

  • The team owns acceptance; "by reason" is the working default, and the reasoning for every decision is visible in its ADR.
  • Deadlock has a predictable exit — Technical Lead, then Head of Dev — so no decision can stall the project indefinitely, while the ladder's rare use keeps authority from becoming the mechanism.
  • Each use of the escalation ladder is itself recorded in the deciding ADR, keeping even the exceptions legible.
  • This ADR decides the governance principle; concrete rituals (where discussion happens, how "no consensus" is declared, review cadence) are refined in follow-up work as the practice matures.
