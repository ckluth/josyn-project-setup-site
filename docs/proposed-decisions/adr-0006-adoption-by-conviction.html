ADR-0006: Adoption by conviction

  • Date: 2026-07-18
  • Status: proposed
  • Promoted from: Idea 0002: Adoption by conviction
  • Tags: culture, change-management, psychology

Context

The non-negotiables — agentic and decision-driven — must be adopted by genuine conviction, not decree. The task is to win buy-in across three audiences that start from different stances:

  • the experienced architect-mate — a peer to keep fully in the loop;
  • the 2 young architects — possible future owners, to empower;
  • the 4 technical leads (incl. the "leads-lead") and the department head — peers and authority carrying the weight of the traditional way, to convince and keep aligned.

The driver can have the last word but does not want anyone overruled or left unconvinced. A culture adopted by reason is the only kind that survives the founder's eventual exit; imposed practice would collapse the moment he steps back. This ADR settles how conviction is won — the mechanism, not a schedule.

Decision Drivers

  • Conviction must be genuine — imposed practice collapses once the founder steps back, defeating the whole purpose.
  • The driver will not overrule or leave anyone unconvinced — reason, not decree.
  • Different audiences respond to different levers; one pitch does not fit all.
  • Early credibility is fragile — over-promising and then stumbling discredits the entire shift.
  • Work must never stall waiting for unanimous conviction.

Considered Options

  • A — Tailored, pull-based adoption, won by example and radiating outward. Use the right lever per audience, lead quietly by doing real work through the trail, and let demonstrated success build the case audience by audience.
  • B — Scheduled top-down rollout with a kickoff pitch/demo. Fast and legible, but decree-like; it risks over-promising, and any early stumble discredits the method — the opposite of "by conviction."
  • C — Consensus first: wait until everyone is convinced before adopting. Honest but brittle — a single holdout stalls the whole shift indefinitely.

Why A. It makes conviction genuine and durable by winning it through evidence and participation rather than decree, while never letting a holdout block progress. B inverts the "by reason" culture and over-promises; C has no exit from deadlock.

Proposed decision

  1. Win conviction per audience, with the right lever. Participation for the young architects (empowered as possible future owners); evidence and demonstration for the technical leads and department head; a co-driver partnership for the experienced architect-mate, in the loop from the start.
  2. Lead by example, quietly. No big pitch or staged demo moment — run genuine work through the decision-trail and let the artifacts speak. Deliberately under-promise; let observed value, not claims, do the convincing.
  3. Radiate outward. Start where conviction is easiest — the architect-mate as co-driver together with the young architects — and let demonstrated success build the case before the leads and department head fully adopt.
  4. Coexist with dissent. Keep engaging a genuinely unconvinced peer by argument over time; never force adoption, and rely on accumulating evidence to convince. Invoke the ADR-0003 escalation ladder only if work is actually blocked, not to end disagreement.
  5. Pace is pull-based, not scheduled. Adoption spreads as conviction is won, audience by audience — no fixed rollout date, trading speed for durability.

Consequences

  • The culture rests on reason, so it can outlast the founder's exit — precisely the point; the trade is that adoption is gradual and uneven, with no rollout date.
  • Leading quietly protects early credibility but makes adoption harder to observe or measure — success shows in the trail's actual use, not in announcements.
  • Coexistence tolerates a persistent holdout indefinitely; this is accepted deliberately as the cost of "by reason," with the ADR-0003 escalation ladder as the bounded backstop if, and only if, work blocks.
  • This ADR decides the adoption stance; concrete tactics — which work to showcase, how exactly to involve each person, when to widen the circle — are refined as the practice matures.
